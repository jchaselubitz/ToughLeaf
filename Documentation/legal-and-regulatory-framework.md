## 1. Federal prime contracts: Small Business Act and FAR

The main framework is:

- Small Business Act §8(d), 15 U.S.C. §637(d)
- FAR Subpart 19.7
- FAR 52.219-8 and 52.219-9
- 13 CFR §125.3

For a federal construction contract exceeding $2 million that offers meaningful subcontracting opportunities, a large prime contractor generally must submit an approved small-business subcontracting plan. The plan must establish separate goals for:

- Small businesses generally
- Small disadvantaged businesses
- Women-owned small businesses
- HUBZone businesses
- Veteran-owned small businesses
- Service-disabled veteran-owned small businesses

The contractor must also describe its outreach, recordkeeping, reporting, payment practices, and procedures for creating “maximum practicable” opportunities. Similar requirements can flow down to large first-tier subcontractors receiving construction subcontracts over $2 million.  

This is usually a planning and good-faith obligation, not an absolute quota. A contractor can face poor performance evaluations, breach findings, liquidated damages, or other remedies for failing to make a good-faith effort—not merely because it missed a numerical target.  

## 2. Federally funded state and local projects: Uniform Guidance

Projects funded through many federal grants are governed by 2 CFR §200.321.

Recipients and subrecipients are expected, when possible, to ensure consideration of:

- Small businesses
- Minority-owned businesses
- Women-owned businesses
- Veteran-owned businesses
- Businesses in labor-surplus areas

Required affirmative steps can include placing these firms on solicitation lists, actively soliciting them, dividing work into smaller packages, adjusting schedules where practical, and requiring prime contractors to take the same steps.  

This provision often appears in federally funded municipal construction, infrastructure, disaster-recovery, school, housing, and public-facility contracts.

## 3. Transportation projects: DOT DBE program

Highway, transit, and airport projects receiving FHWA, FTA, or FAA assistance are governed by 49 CFR Part 26, the Disadvantaged Business Enterprise program.

A funding recipient may establish project-specific DBE participation goals. When a goal applies, a bidder must generally either:

1. Commit sufficient work to certified DBEs to meet the goal, or
2. Document adequate good-faith efforts to do so.

Required documentation can include DBE firms contacted, scopes offered, quotations received, and explanations for selecting a non-DBE bid. Failure to comply can lead to rejection of the bid, withheld payments, sanctions, liquidated damages, termination, or disqualification.  

A significant change occurred on October 3, 2025: DOT removed automatic race- and sex-based presumptions of disadvantage. The current program is expressly supposed to operate without regard to race or sex, and certification now requires an individualized showing of disadvantage. DOT also instructed recipients to remove or zero out certain previously advertised DBE goals while programs were reevaluated.  

So older explanations describing the DOT program simply as a minority- and women-owned subcontracting mandate are now materially outdated.

## 4. HUD-funded housing and public construction: Section 3

Section 3 of the Housing and Urban Development Act, implemented through 24 CFR Part 75, applies to certain HUD-funded housing rehabilitation, housing construction, and other public construction.

It requires recipients to make efforts to direct employment and economic opportunities toward low- and very-low-income workers and qualifying “Section 3 business concerns.” Measures can include outreach, technical assistance, smaller bid packages, and bonding assistance.  

It does not guarantee a subcontract to a Section 3 business, and the regulation expressly says it does not require contracting or subcontracting with one. It is principally an effort, prioritization, benchmarking, and reporting regime.  

## 5. State and municipal MBE/WBE/SBE programs

Many states, cities, transit agencies, housing authorities, school districts, and public-development authorities impose their own programs, commonly using labels such as:

- MBE: Minority Business Enterprise
- WBE: Women Business Enterprise
- SBE: Small Business Enterprise
- DBE: Disadvantaged Business Enterprise
- VBE or SDVBE: Veteran or service-disabled veteran business
- LBE: Local Business Enterprise

These may require:

- Project-specific percentage goals
- Good-faith outreach
- Submission of subcontractor utilization schedules
- Use of certified businesses
- Approval before replacing a listed subcontractor
- Monthly utilization and payment reports
- Prompt payment
- Commercially useful function verification

Their legal authority generally comes from state procurement statutes, municipal ordinances, agency regulations, funding agreements, and the particular solicitation and prime contract.

Race- and sex-conscious public programs face constitutional constraints. Under cases such as City of Richmond v. Croson and Adarand Constructors v. Peña, explicit racial classifications generally receive strict judicial scrutiny. Governments usually need evidence of discrimination, narrow tailoring, periodic review, waivers or good-faith alternatives, and a close connection to the relevant market.  

## 6. Private construction projects

Purely private construction projects usually have no general statutory duty to use small, minority-owned, or women-owned subcontractors.

An obligation can still arise from:

- Development agreements with a city
- Public financing, tax credits, grants, or subsidies
- Land-disposition agreements
- Community-benefits agreements
- Owner-imposed supplier-diversity requirements
- Loan or investor conditions
- Contractual commitments made during zoning or permitting
- Projects on public land

In those cases, the legally enforceable requirement may be in the development agreement, funding document, procurement manual, or prime contract rather than in a generally applicable statute.

## Practical distinction

There are three different levels of obligation that are often conflated:

1. Mandatory utilization: A specific certified subcontractor or percentage must be used, subject to waivers or approved substitutions.
2. Goal plus good-faith efforts: The contractor may miss the percentage but must prove serious efforts.
3. Affirmative consideration: The contractor must solicit, divide packages, advertise, document outreach, and avoid excluding qualifying firms, but no award is guaranteed.

Most federal rules fall primarily into the second or third categories. The exact obligations are usually determined by the project’s funding source, solicitation clauses, contract amount, location, and whether the prime contractor is itself a small business.
