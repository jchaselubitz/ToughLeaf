## Week one: turning the contract into an operating system

| Day | What you see |
|---|---|
| Monday | I read the solicitation, award, amendments, subcontracting plan, utilization commitments, funding documents, schedule, and flow-down clauses. I create an obligation map: requirement, source, applicable firms, target or benchmark, evidence required, owner, deadline, and reporting destination. |
| Tuesday | I build the subcontractor baseline. For every firm, I record trade, scope, contract value, tier, certification, issuing body, expiration date, ownership/control evidence where relevant, and whether the firm is actually performing the work it is being credited for. |
| Wednesday | I hold onboarding calls with subcontractors. Much of the work is explaining exactly what must be submitted, when it is due, who can sign it, and what happens if the firm’s certification expires or its scope changes. I also discover that small firms often lack a dedicated compliance administrator. |
| Thursday | I reconstruct the outreach record. Who was contacted? When? For what scope? Was the package too large for smaller firms? Were schedules or bonding requirements realistic? Which firms quoted? Why was another firm selected? The evidence of good-faith effort matters as much as the final percentage. |
| Friday | I reconcile commitments against reality: awarded subcontract dollars, approved changes, work performed, invoices, payments, retainage, and current participation. The key lesson is that “committed,” “performed,” “invoiced,” and “paid” are different numbers. |

You would see me constantly asking:

- What is the denominator?
- Which date controls the firm’s status?
- Is this a legal requirement, a contract promise, a goal, or an agency reporting convention?
- Is the firm certified by the correct authority for this project?
- Can we prove what happened six months from now?

For a federal construction contract, for example, the current FAR threshold for a subcontracting plan is generally $2 million when subcontracting opportunities exist, and the plan is not required from a small-business prime. The plan includes goals, payment assurances, outreach, recordkeeping, and reporting obligations. [FAR 19.702](https://www.acquisition.gov/far/19.702) [FAR 52.219-9](https://login.acquisition.gov/far/52.219-9)

## Week two: managing exceptions before they become findings

The second week is less orderly because live projects generate problems.

### Monday: the dashboard review

I start with exceptions, not with a summary percentage:

- certification expiring in 14 days;
- subcontractor working before onboarding is complete;
- missing payroll or workforce data;
- participation below the projected curve;
- payment made late or for less than the approved amount;
- unapproved subcontractor substitution;
- change order moving work away from a credited firm;
- agency report due soon;
- evidence missing for a past decision.

A green percentage can conceal a serious problem. A project can be above its participation target but still fail because the wrong firm performed the work, required approval was missing, or the evidence cannot be produced.

### Tuesday: the project meeting

You sit with the project manager, estimator, procurement team, accounts payable, and superintendent. I am not popular when a schedule change creates a compliance problem.

A project manager says:

> “The listed electrical subcontractor cannot staff the job. We need to replace them this week.”

I ask:

1. What does the contract require before replacement?
2. Has the owner or agency approved the substitution?
3. Why did the original firm fail?
4. Was the replacement firm properly certified?
5. Does the replacement perform the required scope itself?
6. What outreach or good-faith effort was made?
7. What happens to the original commitment and the report already submitted?

I do not simply block the project. I create a controlled decision: document the reason, preserve the evidence, seek approval where required, identify a qualified replacement, update the commitment, and track the remediation.

### Wednesday: workforce and payroll

This is where the word “participation” becomes more complicated.

On a HUD Section 3 project, for example, the focus may be employment, training, and labor-hour benchmarks—not simply the percentage of subcontract dollars. HUD’s current rule prioritizes Section 3 workers and Section 3 business concerns, and its safe harbor uses labor-hour ratios plus certification that the required prioritization efforts were followed. [24 CFR 75.19](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/subtitle-A/part-75/subpart-C/section-75.19) [24 CFR 75.23](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-24/subtitle-A/part-75/subpart-C/section-75.23)

So I compare:

- worker identity and eligibility documentation;
- employer and subcontractor;
- project and work location;
- hours worked;
- payroll or time records;
- training or targeted-worker status;
- duplicate or implausible entries;
- the reporting period used by the agency.

You see a lot of careful reconciliation and a lot of conversations about privacy. Workforce information is sensitive; the system should expose compliance status without exposing more personal data than necessary.

### Thursday: payments

I match subcontract agreements, approved change orders, invoices, payment applications, waivers, payment dates, retainage, and actual cash disbursements.

The compliance question is not merely “was this firm awarded work?” It is also “was the firm paid in accordance with the contract?” For federal contracts containing a subcontracting plan, FAR requires assurances concerning timely payment and notification to the contracting officer if a small subcontractor is paid late or at a reduced amount. [FAR 52.219-9](https://login.acquisition.gov/far/52.219-9)

This is where compliance becomes financially consequential. A missing document may delay a payment. A payment delay may damage a small firm’s ability to staff the project. A late change in scope may create both a cash-flow problem and a participation shortfall.

### Friday: reporting and audit preparation

I prepare the report from the underlying evidence rather than manually typing totals into a form.

For a federal individual subcontracting plan, the contractor submits Individual Subcontract Reports and Summary Subcontract Reports through eSRS. ISRs are generally submitted semiannually and at contract completion, with reports due 30 days after the reporting period. [FAR 52.219-9](https://login.acquisition.gov/far/52.219-9)

The report itself is the last step. Before submitting, I want to answer:

- Can every dollar be traced to a subcontract?
- Can every credited firm be traced to a valid certification?
- Can every workforce hour be traced to a source record?
- Can every substitution be traced to approval or documented justification?
- Can every payment be traced to an invoice and date?
- Can we explain variance from the original plan?
- Would another person understand the file without calling me?

## The regulatory complication you would notice

The same words can mean different things in different regimes.

- Under federal prime-contract rules, the emphasis may be a subcontracting plan, socioeconomic categories, maximum practicable opportunity, good-faith effort, payment, and eSRS reporting.
- Under a federally funded state or local project, the emphasis may be affirmative procurement steps such as solicitation lists, smaller bid packages, realistic schedules, and flow-down obligations. [2 CFR 200.321](https://www.ecfr.gov/current/title-2/subtitle-A/chapter-II/part-200/subpart-D/section-200.321)
- Under DOT’s DBE program, the operating environment changed materially in October 2025. DOT removed race- and sex-based presumptions of disadvantage and required programs to operate without regard to race or sex, with individualized certification and transitional implementation issues. [DOT DBE program updates](https://www.transportation.gov/DBEFinalRule) [October 2025 Interim Final Rule](https://www.transportation.gov/sites/dot.gov/files/2025-10/DBE%20IFR.Published.10-03-2025.pdf)
- Under HUD Section 3, the relevant measure may be labor hours and prioritization of opportunities rather than a simple subcontracting percentage.
- Under a private project, the requirement may exist only because of a development agreement, subsidy, loan condition, owner requirement, or prime contract.

A senior compliance officer therefore distrusts any system that labels a project simply “compliant.” The real status is more like:

> Compliant with which obligation, for which reporting period, based on which evidence, under whose interpretation, and with what unresolved exceptions?

## What would surprise you most

You would probably expect me to spend the day finding diverse subcontractors. In practice, that is only one part of the lifecycle, and often belongs before ClearComply begins. The larger operational burden is keeping the original commitment intact while construction changes around it.

You would also see that my most valuable work is not rejecting documents. It is preventing ambiguity:

- defining requirements before work begins;
- making the subcontractor understand them;
- catching changes early;
- preserving the reason for each decision;
- escalating the few issues that require human judgment;
- producing a defensible record without a last-minute reconstruction.

If I were translating the shadowing experience into product requirements, I would want ClearComply to provide five things:

1. A project-specific obligation map.
2. A single subcontractor and certification record with history and expiration tracking.
3. Event-driven workflows for onboarding, substitutions, changes, payments, reporting, and closeout.
4. Reconciliation between commitments, work performed, payroll, invoices, and payments.
5. An exception queue where every alert has an owner, deadline, severity, and evidence trail.

The central product insight is this: compliance is not a document repository with reminders. It is a time-sensitive evidence system connected to project decisions.
